Rationale

The thinking behind this portfolio

This portfolio is part of CRS514 and it covers my development as a pre-service teacher of Stage 6 Mathematics. I'm in the middle of that journey — I haven't taught a Stage 6 class yet, but I've spent four weeks at Merrylands High School teaching Year 8 and Year 9, and that experience changed how I think about what it means to actually stand in front of students and try to make maths make sense to them.

I came into this degree from an IT background, so I lean toward technology instinctively. But Merrylands knocked some of the confidence out of that. You can build a perfectly designed Desmos activity and it still won't work if the students don't trust you, or if half the class can't read the instructions because English isn't their first language. Chechan et al. (2023) found real gains when ICT was woven properly into a lesson sequence — but the "properly" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, and I'm still figuring out what that looks like in practice.

The portfolio is framed around the APST Graduate Standards (AITSL, 2011). Some artefacts here are Stage 6 resources I've developed as part of this course — things I intend to use when I teach HSC classes. Others come directly from Merrylands. Both matter. The placement gave me real classroom experience; the Stage 6 resources show where I'm heading. Li (2025) makes the point that reflection has to be tied to something specific to actually mean anything, and I've tried to do that here — both with the placement artefacts and with the ones I'm still building toward.

Learning Goals

Five things I'm working toward as a Stage 6 Mathematics teacher

1

Build solid content knowledge across the HSC syllabuses

Stage 6 maths — Advanced, Extension 1, Extension 2 — goes to places I need to prepare for carefully. I'm reasonably confident across most of the Advanced content but I know there are gaps, particularly in Extension 2 topics like proof and complex numbers. Schüler-Meyer et al. (2024) makes the point that you can't teach what you don't deeply understand — so this is ongoing, not something I tick off once and move on from.

2

Use technology for a reason, not as a habit

My IT background makes me reach for tools like Desmos and GeoGebra almost automatically. That's not always a good thing. Merrylands taught me that tech only helps when the classroom conditions support it. Chechan et al. (2023) found significant learning gains when ICT was genuinely embedded in a lesson sequence — not just shown, but built in. That's what I want my Stage 6 lessons to look like.

APST 2.6.1Desmos · GeoGebraICT Integration
3

Differentiate properly across a Stage 6 class

Stage 6 students self-select into courses, but there's still a real range inside each class. Merrylands gave me my first taste of this — I had Year 9 students who were years apart in readiness, and I had to work out how to design tasks that reached everyone. Insorio (2024) describe enabling and extending prompts as a way to do this without giving different students different mathematics, and that's the approach I want to take into Stage 6.

APST 1.3.1APST 3.4.1Differentiation
4

Give feedback that moves students forward, not just backward-facing marks

When I marked student work at Merrylands and wrote just a score, students looked at the number and put the paper in their bag. When I added a note about what to do differently, a few of them actually read it. Andersson and Palm (2023) call the second type feed-forward and it's what I want my Stage 6 assessment practice to be built around.

APST 5.1.1APST 5.3.1Feedback
5

Reflect specifically, not generally

I kept a journal at Merrylands and the early entries were useless — vague impressions, no specifics. The entries got better once my mentors started giving me concrete feedback I could actually respond to. Li (2025) says reflection only works when it's tied to a real moment, not a general feeling. That's the habit I want to carry into Stage 6 teaching.

Artefact 1 — ICT Integration · Stage 6 (Planned)
Artefact 1
Desmos Activity — Transformations of Functions (Year 11 Advanced)
1
APST 2.6.1 Stage 6 — Planned
DesmosICT IntegrationFunctionsYear 11 Advanced
I built this Desmos activity during CRS514 as a resource I plan to use when I teach Year 11 Advanced Mathematics. The activity lets students drag sliders for a, h and k in y = af(x − h) + k and watch the graph shift in real time, rather than copying three static examples from the board. The idea is that students will actually see what each parameter does, not just memorise what they're supposed to do. I haven't used this in a Stage 6 classroom yet, but it's grounded in Chechan et al. (2023), who found measurable gains when technology was built properly into a lesson sequence rather than used as a demonstration.
Dragging the slider for a, h or k moves the teal curve in real time — students see the effect of each parameter as they adjust it
📚
Research Annotation

Chechan et al. (2023) found effect sizes of .50 to .63 across three large studies when technology was properly embedded in instructional sequences. For transformations, the ability to manipulate a parameter and see the result immediately supports the kind of conceptual understanding that static diagrams on a board can't easily produce. I've also designed a written generalisation task to follow the exploration — based on Pope (2023), who warn that digital tools can let students coast without actually thinking unless the task demands a conclusion.

My Reflection

I haven't taught this in a Stage 6 class yet, so I can't speak to how it actually lands with Year 11 students. What I can say is that building it made me think carefully about the sequencing — when in the lesson to introduce the sliders, how long to leave before the generalisation task, what to do when a student just drags everything at random without noticing anything. Those are questions I'll only really answer the first time I try it. I'd plan a post-lesson reflection journal entry immediately after the first time I teach it.

Artefact 2 — Content Knowledge · Stage 6 (Planned)
Artefact 2
Stage 6 Calculus Scope & Sequence — Year 11–12 Advanced
2
APST 2.1.1 Stage 6 — Planned
CalculusScope & SequenceYear 11–12 AdvancedCurriculum Planning
This is a planning resource I've developed as preparation for teaching Stage 6 — a scope and sequence mapping how the calculus strand runs across Year 11 and Year 12 Advanced Mathematics, identifying where the prerequisite dependencies sit and which topics students typically struggle with when they haven't fully consolidated what came before. I built this during CRS514 to develop the kind of content sequencing knowledge that Schüler-Meyer et al. (2024) calls pedagogical content knowledge — understanding not just what the content is, but the order that makes it learnable and the points where it usually breaks down.
Mapping the calculus strand helps me plan which Year 11 gaps to close before they become Year 12 problems
📚
Research Annotation

Schüler-Meyer et al. (2024) argued that effective teachers hold a specific kind of knowledge beyond content mastery — they understand which representations work best for which concepts and which prior ideas students must have in place before a new topic will land. In Stage 6 calculus, this matters enormously: a student who doesn't understand function notation in Year 11 will struggle with integration in Year 12, but the connection isn't always obvious without a deliberate map. This scope and sequence is my attempt to build that map before I need it in front of a class.

My Reflection

I built this working from the NESA syllabus documents, and I found it genuinely useful — drawing out the dependency chain made me notice that differentiation rules are doing a lot of heavy lifting as a prerequisite, which isn't always obvious just from reading the outcomes list. What I'll only know once I start teaching Stage 6 is which gaps actually appear most often in real students, versus which ones I've predicted based on theory. I plan to revisit and annotate this map with real observations after my first year in the job.

Artefact 3 — Differentiation · Stage 5 (From Placement)
Artefact 3
Tiered Worksheets — Linear Equations, Year 9 (Merrylands HS)
3
APST 3.4.1 From Placement
DifferentiationEAL/DMerrylands HSYear 9 · Stage 5
This came directly out of a problem I ran into at Merrylands. My 9Maths5 class had students who weren't yet confident with basic two-step arithmetic sitting alongside students who could already handle multi-step algebra — and a large chunk of the class were EAL/D learners who needed clearer language, not harder maths. I built three versions of the same worksheet: scaffolded with a worked example and guided steps, standard with independent practice, and extension with multi-step reasoning questions. Insorio (2024) call this enabling and extending prompts — I came across the term after I'd already tried the approach, and the research confirmed what I'd worked out through trial and error.
Same learning outcome, three entry points — nobody gets easier maths, they just get a different door into the same room
📚
Research Annotation

Insorio (2024) argue that the goal of differentiation isn't to give different students different mathematics — it's to give every student access to the same mathematical thinking, through tasks that have multiple entry points. The enabling prompt reduces the barrier for students who need it; the extending prompt keeps the ceiling high for students who are ready. I was trying to do exactly this at Merrylands, and the research gives me a framework to refine it further when I move into Stage 6 classes.

My Reflection

The first version of the scaffolded worksheet was too easy — the jump from the scaffolded tier to the standard tier was too big, and some students stalled when they moved across. My mentor spotted it before I did and pointed it out directly. I revised it, tightened the bridge between tiers, and the second version worked considerably better. The lesson for Stage 6 is the same: check that the tiers actually connect before the lesson, not during it.

Artefact 4 — Formative Assessment · Stage 6 (Planned)
Artefact 4
Formative Assessment Toolkit — Stage 6 Mathematics
4
APST 5.1.1 Stage 6 — Planned
Formative AssessmentExit TicketsKahootStage 6
This is a toolkit of five low-stakes formative assessment strategies I've designed to use in Stage 6 classes: diagnostic entrance tickets at the start of a lesson, mini-whiteboard responses mid-explanation, Kahoot quizzes, traffic light self-rating cards, and peer explanation protocols. I developed it during CRS514 in preparation for teaching Stage 6. Some of these I trialled in a limited way at Merrylands — the whiteboard check in particular was immediately useful — but this toolkit is designed with the Stage 6 context in mind, where the HSC creates pressure that can push teaching toward test practice at the expense of genuine understanding. Andersson and Palm (2023) give the strongest research case for why formative practice matters, particularly for students who are already behind.
All five are low-stakes — they tell me who's lost in the next five minutes, not just after the exam
📚
Research Annotation

Andersson and Palm (2023) reviewed 250 studies and found that formative assessment — when implemented with genuine rigour — produces significant achievement gains, with stronger effects for lower-achieving students. In Stage 6, the temptation is to treat every lesson as HSC exam practice, which leaves almost no room for catching and correcting misunderstandings before they compound. This toolkit is designed to build a formative culture alongside the HSC preparation, not instead of it.

My Reflection

I tried the mini-whiteboard strategy at Merrylands and it was immediately the most useful thing I did for gauging the room. Thirty seconds to see who's with me and who's lost, without anyone feeling put on the spot. I haven't tried this toolkit in a Stage 6 context yet, and I'm conscious that Year 11 and 12 students might respond differently — more self-conscious about showing gaps in front of peers, or more focused on marks than understanding. Those are things I'll need to manage when I try this for real.

Artefact 5 — Feedback · Stage 6 (Planned)
Artefact 5
Annotated Marking Guide — Year 12 Advanced Trial Exam
5
APST 5.3.1 Stage 6 — Planned
FeedbackMarking GuideYear 12 AdvancedStage 6
I developed this marking guide template during CRS514 as a resource I intend to use when marking Year 12 Advanced trial examinations. Instead of just allocating marks, it includes a "common error" field and a "next step" prompt for each question — so when students get their paper back, they receive something they can actually act on before the HSC, not just a number. This is based on what Andersson and Palm (2023) call feed-forward feedback, and I already started applying the principle at Merrylands, though there in a Stage 5 context. The Stage 6 version of this is designed with the HSC timeline in mind — feedback that arrives in time to actually change something.
Students get the mark either way — the right-hand version gives them something to do with it before the HSC
📚
Research Annotation

Andersson and Palm (2023) found an effect size of 0.79 for feedback that tells students how to improve — compared to much weaker effects for feedback that only tells them how they went. In Stage 6, where students have limited time between trial exams and the HSC, the question isn't just "what did they get wrong?" but "can they fix it in time?" A marking guide that answers that question for every question on the paper is designed to make that fix as straightforward as possible.

My Reflection

I started doing a simpler version of this at Merrylands — writing brief notes on Year 9 tests rather than just ticking and crossing. It took around three times as long to mark, and I underestimated that the first time. Students did engage with the notes more than I expected, though — a few came back to ask about them the next lesson, which hadn't happened when I just returned the scored paper. For Stage 6, I'm planning to use a bank of pre-written common error notes that I can insert quickly, rather than writing each comment from scratch, which should reduce the marking time significantly.

This is a selection of entries from the reflective journal I kept across my four weeks at Merrylands. I wrote entries as close to immediately after lessons as I could manage — not at the end of the day when the details had gone fuzzy. The entries cover Year 8 and Year 9 Mathematics classes and document specific moments: what happened in a lesson, what I'd change, what my mentor said. Research Annotation

Li (2025) makes the case that reflective practice is only productive when it examines taken-for-granted assumptions — the things you do automatically, without consciously deciding to. At Merrylands, my mentor teachers helped me see several of those: assumptions about pacing, about who was following, about what "explaining clearly" actually requires. Li (2025) identify sustained mentoring with targeted feedback as the most effective form of early-career professional learning, and my journal captures that process across the four weeks.

My Reflection

Looking back at Week 1 entries now is uncomfortable — they say almost nothing useful. "Students were engaged, lesson flowed well." That's not reflection, it's reassurance. The entries got genuinely useful around Week 3, once I stopped trying to summarise everything and started writing about one specific thing that happened and what it meant. The trigger was my mentor asking "why do you think that happened?" after a lesson that didn't go as planned, and me not having a good answer. I plan to carry that specific question into my Stage 6 teaching and keep asking it.

🏫 Professional Experience

Merrylands High School

Term 2, 2026  ·  Year 8 & Year 9 Mathematics  ·  4 Weeks

I spent four weeks at Merrylands High School in Term 2, 2026, teaching two Year 9 classes (9Maths5) and one Year 8 class (8MAR). It's a school with a lot of EAL/D students and a wide range of family backgrounds, mostly low-income, and one of my Year 8 students had more than one disability that needed real consideration in how I planned. Four weeks isn't long — I was still getting properly comfortable with the staff and the school's rhythms by the time it ended — but it was enough to learn some things the hard way that I don't think I would have learned from a textbook.

The achievement spread in 9Maths5 in particular meant I couldn't get away with a single version of any task, which is where Insorio (2024)'s ideas about tiered prompts stopped being theory and started being something I actually had to use, fast.

Mentors who told me the truth instead of just saying "good job." Watching other teachers and asking why they did what they did. And honestly, just the students — when something finally clicked for one of them, that's the moment that made the whole four weeks worth it.

✦ Critical Reflection

Self-Reflection

An honest look at where I'm at, not a polished version of it

1

What four weeks at Merrylands actually taught me

Going in, I assumed the hard part of teaching would be the maths itself — explaining things clearly, getting the sequencing right. That part was mostly fine. What actually challenged me was the sheer range inside one classroom. In 9Maths5 I had students who still weren't fully confident with basic number facts sitting two seats from students who could handle two-step algebra without blinking, and I had to keep adjusting in real time, which no lesson plan, however detailed, fully prepares you for. The tiered worksheets I describe in Artefact 3 came directly out of trying to solve that problem, and they weren't right the first time I tried them.

The single most useful thing about placement was my mentors refusing to just tell me I was doing fine. They'd point out a specific moment — a question I'd rushed past, an explanation that lost half the room — and that kind of targeted, sometimes uncomfortable feedback is exactly what Li (2025) say actually moves early-career teachers forward, more than any generic professional development session would.

2

What this portfolio says about where I'm at

I didn't pick these six artefacts because they're my best work — I picked them because I can actually talk about each one honestly, including the parts that didn't go smoothly the first time. That's closer to what Li (2025) means by reflective practice than a polished showcase would be. Putting this together made it pretty clear that I'm solid on planning and on the ICT side of things, and considerably less solid on the in-the-moment stuff — reading a room properly, adjusting an explanation on the fly when I can see it's not landing, building enough trust with a class that a confused student will actually put their hand up.

3

Where I need to go from here

Three things, realistically. My content knowledge across the Stage 5 curriculum still has gaps — I know the core topics well but some of the geometry and probability strands are less developed, which matters when an unexpected student question pushes into territory I haven't fully prepared. I need to work on parent and carer engagement, which barely came up in four weeks and is a real blind spot. And I want to get better at giving useful feedback in the moment rather than relying on written comments after the fact — something Andersson and Palm (2023) identify as one of the higher-impact habits to build. None of this is finished. That's the point of a portfolio like this — it's a record of where I am, not where I'll end up.
